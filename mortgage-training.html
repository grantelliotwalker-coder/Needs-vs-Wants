Strategic mortgages Theory
Use this page as a training guide to understand how rates, term, and surplus planning fit together.

A mortgage is more than a repayment amount; it is a long‑term contract where small changes in rate, term, or extra repayments can shift total interest by tens of thousands.

This guide mirrors how the calculator works so you can see how the numbers connect to real‑world strategy, including your budget surplus and the impact of future rate changes.

How the repayment formula works Formula
The training uses the same standard amortising loan formula as the calculator.

Most repayment calculators, including your main mortgage tool, use a standard amortising loan formula. This ensures each regular payment covers the interest due plus a slice of principal so the loan finishes on time.

The core idea is that the payment is set so that the present value of all payments equals the starting loan amount, given the interest rate and number of payments.

Repayment formula (conceptual)

Payment = principal × r × (1 + r)^n ---------------------------------- (1 + r)^n − 1 Where: principal = loan amount after deposit r = periodic interest rate (e.g. annual rate / 12 for monthly) n = total number of payments (years × payments per year)

When rates rise, the r term increases, which pushes repayments higher and also raises total interest, because more of each payment goes to interest instead of principal at the start.

Strategy: using your surplus Planning
Link your mortgage to the budget surplus concept used in the main planner.

The main planner stores a weekly surplus, which represents the gap between income and regular spending. Your mortgage share is then compared against this surplus to show how comfortable the repayment is.

A smaller ratio between your mortgage share and your surplus generally means more resilience to rate rises, job changes, or other shocks.

Comfort zones (idea)

  • Comfortable: mortgage share uses a relatively small slice of your surplus, leaving room for savings and buffers.
  • OK: mortgage share uses a moderate part of the surplus, still workable but with less flexibility.
  • Tight: most of the surplus is consumed by the mortgage, leaving limited room for changes.

In the main calculator, this logic is expressed through coverage labels and a progress bar comparing your mortgage share to your stored surplus.

Extra repayments and time saved Tips
Small regular extra payments can shorten the term and reduce total interest.

When you add extra repayments, more principal is paid off each period, which reduces the interest charged next period, creating a compounding effect in your favour.

The main calculator simulates this by re‑running the repayment schedule with the extra amount added to each payment and then measuring the new payoff time and total interest.

Tips when using the main tool

  • Experiment with extra repayments that match realistic surplus, not just theoretical maximums.
  • Compare the “base” term against the “with extras” term to see time saved in years and months.
  • Use future rate scenarios to check if your extra repayments still keep the mortgage comfortable under higher rates.
